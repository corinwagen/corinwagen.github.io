Corin Wagen

About

I'm a graduate student in the Jacobsen group at Harvard, where I study the development and mechanism of selective catalytic reactions.

I'm generally interested in problems at the interface of organic, computational, and analytical chemistry, with an emphasis on developing computational techniques that enable practical advances. I also like thinking critically about how the broader research landscape could be improved.

From 2016 to 2019, I was an undergraduate at MIT, where I majored in chemistry and studied the design of new phosphine ligands in the Buchwald group.

Current Projects

"Screening for Generality" in Asymmetric Catalysis

Most asymmetric reactions are optimized using a single model substrate, generating methods that often work only for a narrow range of substrates. While screening on multiple substrates might yield more general transformations, existing enantiodetermination methods require substrate-specific optimization, making this strategy too costly to be practical. By replacing SFC-UV with SFC-MS, we were able to measure the enantioselectivity of mixtures of crude products with acceptable error, enabling rapid multi-substrate screening. We surveyed the generality of reported Brønsted-acid catalysts for the Pictet–Spengler reaction and found that SPINOL-derived chiral phosphoric acids gave promising results for a wide variety of substrates. (preprint on Chemrxiv; with Eugene Kwan and Spencer McMinn)

Mechanistic Study of Enantioselective Brønsted Acid Catalysis

Recent results from our group indicate that chiral hydrogen-bond-donor catalysts can act cooperatively with strong achiral Brønsted acids to promote challenging acid-catalyzed reactions with high enantioselectivity. In particular, we found that hydrogen-bond donors lead to a 93x increase in the rate of an HCl-catalyzed Prins reaction, giving product in up to 98% ee. Surprisingly, in situ pKa measurements revealed that the catalyst actually decreases the acidity of HCl by forming a catalyst•HCl complex; rate acceleration comes not from enhancement of acidity but through catalyst control of chloride positioning, which creates an electric field that stabilizes electron flow in the major transition state. (preprint on Chemrxiv; with Dennis Kutateladze)

presto: Efficient and Modular QM/QM' Molecular Dynamics

Although quantum mechanical models have been applied to molecular dynamics for decades, the immense computing power now available makes this feasible on an unpredecented scale. By using an ONIOM-based approach that combines density-functional theory and Stefan Grimme's xtb semiempirical methods, we have been able to simulate large systems (over 1000 atoms in the "low" level) for tens of picoseconds. This approach can be combined with existing biased-sampling techniques like WHAM, enabling the construction of solvated free energy surfaces for complex transformations. For more details, see the website.

Past Work

cctk: computational chemistry toolkit

cctk is a Python library which automates routine tasks in computational chemistry, such as geometric perturbations, file type conversion, and property extraction. This enables simultaneous monitoring and analysis of thousands of jobs, greatly increasing computational throughput, and serves as the foundation for my other computational projects. For more details, see the website. (with Eugene Kwan)

Triaryl Ligand Design

Although C–F reductive elimination from Pd(II) was long thought to be impossible, the triaryl ligand AlPhos renders this step facile even at room temperature. We studied this unique ligand by synthesizing a library of analogues and measuring their initial rates for a model fluorination reaction. Our data demonstrate that the ortho-fluorines on the bottom ring are crucial for reactivity, consistent with the hypothesis that F–F repulsion destabilizes the Pd(II) fluoride intermediate and promotes reductive elimination. (with Bryan Ingoglia, results published in his thesis)
